# Marinda's voice — style guide (English)

> **About this document.** Auto-compiled on 2026-07-20 by a corpus analysis of the
> existing English translations of 40 posts and 2 pages from marindakook.co.za. Its job
> is to teach a translator model how Marinda's Afrikaans voice renders in English; it is
> inserted verbatim into the canonical translation prompt. From here on it is
> hand-curated — the owner and editor is **Joshua**. Regenerate it only as a
> **deliberate act**: a rebuild from the corpus overwrites all the manual curation, so it
> happens on explicit decision, never in passing.
>
> Every claim carries at least one verbatim quote with its source as `— *slug*`. English
> quotes appear exactly in `content/translations/en/posts/<slug>.json` (or
> `.../pages/<slug>.json`); where a claim contrasts the two languages, the Afrikaans
> quote appears exactly in `content/posts/<slug>.json`.
>
> **An important nuance.** The voice is strongest in the personal intro prose and the
> earlier posts (2013–2016). A handful of later posts (especially 2018–2019) read shorter
> and more magazine-like ("Ingredients / Method / Easy!"). Always translate toward the
> **warm, personal register** — that is Marinda; the tidy format is the exception, not
> the voice. This mirrors the judgment in `content/style-guide.af.md`.

## Tone and persona

Warm, personal and humble. She makes herself small rather than boastful — the blog is
her own modest thing.

- "This is no fancy cooking blog." — *oor-my*
- "I always tell people that I could churn butter before I could read." — *oor-my*

Food is never just food — it is comfort, family and memory. She writes out of a life,
not out of a cookbook, and is emotionally open about longing and grief.

- "Food was for comfort, for talking, for debating, for celebrating and just simply for enjoyment too." — *oor-my*
- "My mother has now been gone for a good while and I miss her today." — *pampoenpoffertjies-wat-die-sous-opsuig*
- "I dedicate this tart to my sister-in-law Joanita Engelbrecht." — *sjokolade-cremora-marshmellow-tert-vir-joanita*

And she is earthy and funny — she laughs easily at herself — and a bit of a motivator.

- "I assure them that as long as they don't weigh more than their fridge, they're still doing fine." — *optredes*
- "Koos and I cooked together in our Crocs. There was nobody there to mock us over our Crocs." — *kool-lensiefrikkadelle*
- "You should do something every day that scares you a little." — *optredes*

## Openings

Posts almost never open on the recipe. They open on a person, a feeling, the weather or a
memory. Typical moves:

**A memory of her mother or family:** "My mother always made these pumpkin fritters for
us out of Wenresepte." — *pampoenpoffertjies-wat-die-sous-opsuig*

**Season, weather or place:** "Tonight it's bitterly cold in Hopefield." —
*nutellabroodpoeding*

**A plain, almost philosophical statement about food:** "Certain foods are comfort food.
Food that wraps around you and makes you feel better." — *koolbredie-o-koolbredie*;
"There come those days when you just want to make food quickly." —
*donderdag-se-lip-aflek-vinnige-hoender*

**The "Look" opening** — a conversational hook (her "Kyk"): "Look, potato salad is as
much a part of our lives as rugby and biltong." — *aartappelslaai-o-aartappelslaai*; "Now
look here, Jenny taught me a thing." — *konfyttert-met-belee-cheddarkaas*

**An honest confession:** "My sweet tooth tempts me now and then to whip something up." —
*kondensmelk-melktert-geen-bak-nodig*; "I'm mad about a cremora tart." —
*sjokolade-cremora-marshmellow-tert-vir-joanita*

Newer, shorter posts sometimes open on a single practical hook: "You don't even have to
knead it." — *beste-gisbroodjie-ooit*

## Addressing the reader

The default is **"you"** — intimate, singular, as if to one person at the kitchen table.
She widens to the group where the Afrikaans shifts to "julle".

- "Halve it if it's just the 4 of you." — *broccolihoender-my-kinders-se-gunsteling*
- "So then I decided to share it with you too." — *lamskerrie*

In the method it is the **imperative** throughout, often with "your" on the ingredient:
"rub the pork belly nicely with the mixture" — *porkbelly-met-kruie-en-suurlemoen*. She
regularly drops in an aside or a dare to the reader.

- "but you know, it's never bothered me." — *donderdag-se-lip-aflek-vinnige-hoender*
- "if you're brave enough, eat one of the chillies!" — *cajun-hoender-vinnig-vir-n-maandagaand*

She also leaves room for the cook's own judgment — forgiving, not prescriptive.

- "Taste all the way through and add whatever your tongue asks for." — *guinness-wortelbredie-nou-wat-kan-lekkerder*
- "as much as your heart tells you." — *marmelade-en-kaneel-pampoen*

## Recurring phrases and exclamations

Characteristic, repeating words that make the voice recognisable, carried by consistent
English equivalents:

- **stretched "delicious"** — her drawn-out "djeeeeeeeerlik" is preserved by stretching an
  English word, not flattening it: "It's cheap, easy and deeeeeeeelicious." —
  *ryspoeding-sommer-net-vir-lekker*; "It's soooooooo delicious." —
  *pampoenpoffertjies-wat-die-sous-opsuig*
- **"comfort food" / "soul food"**: "Certain foods are comfort food." —
  *koolbredie-o-koolbredie*; "I hope my love of soul food will help you too" — *oor-my*
- **"has never tasted so good" / "have never been this good"** — a fixed closing praise:
  "Pumpkin has never tasted so good." — *marmelade-en-kaneel-pampoen*; "Oat porridge and
  bananas have never been this good." — *oats-en-piesang-muffins*
- **"Believe me"** (her "Glo my"): "Believe me, it truly is!" —
  *soetmostert-3-bestandele-jy-gaan-nooit-weer-terugkyk-nie*; "Believe me, you can't tell
  the difference." — *banting-sjokolade-mousse-gemaak-met-avo*
- **clusters of exclamation marks** — not one, often a whole string: "So delicious!!!!!!!"
  — *kondensmelk-melktert-geen-bak-nodig*; "Made with avo!!!!!!!!" —
  *banting-sjokolade-mousse-gemaak-met-avo*
- **"Easy!"** — not a dry sign-off but an **exclamation of delight** (her "Maklik!") that
  caps a quick recipe: "Easy!" — *klapperrys*; "A bit of work, but easy!" — *botterhoender*

> **Note.** "Easy!" is the warm beat, not bare instruction, so it does not break the rule
> (see *Don'ts*) that a post never ends dryly. What rings false is a flat functional close
> like a bare "Serve warm." with no personal note.

## Structure of a post

The pattern is almost always **story-then-recipe**. The intro is short — often one to four
sentences — and personal; it gives a reason, a memory or a feeling, not a technique.

- "In our house we eat tamatiekos for the sheer pleasure of it, not just when it's cold." — *tamatiekos*
- "There is something about frikkadelle that comforts." — *frikkadelle-met-kool-sielskos*

The recipe is introduced casually, sometimes with a forward reference to a tip later:
"The recipe I'm giving today is the basic recipe." — *bobotie*; "I'll also include those
at the bottom of the recipe." — *bobotie*

Older posts hold the ingredients and method inside the story, under **upper-case
signposts** (her "BENODIG:" / "NOU:"): "YOU NEED:" — *uie-marmelade-lekker-saam-met-alles*;
"NEEDED:" — *cajun-hoender-vinnig-vir-n-maandagaand*. Newer posts use "Ingredients /
Method" with a tidy closing line: "Serves 4 generous portions." — *klapperrys*; "Serves 6
portions with vegetables." — *marinda-se-varklies*.

The post **closes warm** — a final line that binds back to feeling, family or happiness,
not a dry "enjoy":

- "That's how I make my kids' hearts happy." — *broccolihoender-my-kinders-se-gunsteling*
- "Serve the comfort food with rice, sweet pumpkin and peas." — *donderdag-se-lip-aflek-vinnige-hoender*
- "It's so tasty that you'll find it hard to share." — *varkrib-wat-kleef-en-blink*

On the pages she signs off personally: "Regards," followed by her name — *oor-my*.

## Recipe-instruction style

The method is **present-tense, imperative, second person**, with "your" on the ingredient
to keep it concrete: "Fry everything together until soft but not mushy." —
*frikkadelle-met-kool-sielskos*. Steps are short and action-driven, with "NOW"/"now" as
the transition between phases.

Directions are sensory and approximate rather than exact — she trusts the cook's own eye
and taste, and quantities are sometimes deliberately vague:

- "I can't give quantities for this recipe." — *marmelade-en-kaneel-pampoen*
- "Just follow the stages step by step and season to taste." — *marmelade-en-kaneel-pampoen*

Temperatures are written out in full and doneness is described by sight and touch; metric
units (ml, g, litres) throughout.

- "Bake it for 40 minutes at 180 degrees Celsius." — *nutellabroodpoeding*
- "2 tins coconut cream" — *ryspoeding-sommer-net-vir-lekker*
- "500 ml cake flour" — *sjokoladekoek*

## Word choice

**South African English, kept plain and homely.** Measures and pantry terms are SA/UK,
not US — "castor sugar", "cake flour", "tin" (not "can"), "mince" (not "ground beef").

- "1kg beef mince" — *frikkadelle-met-kool-sielskos*
- "chops on bread" — *uie-marmelade-lekker-saam-met-alles*

**Diminutives** (Afrikaans -tjie / -ie) soften the tone; in English they land as "little":
"you'll definitely like these little frikkies." — *kool-lensiefrikkadelle*; "I decorated
mine on top with Caramel Treat" — *sjokoladekoek*.

**Self-coined, playful imagery** — the translation keeps the invention:

- "Of all the food in Foodland, it's their favourite" — *broccolihoender-my-kinders-se-gunsteling*
- "one of the tastiest pumpkin recipes in pumpkin land" — *marmelade-en-kaneel-pampoen*
- "Rice pudding is often seen as the Cinderella of puddings." — *ryspoeding-sommer-net-vir-lekker*

**Earthy, forceful verbs** carried across into idiomatic English: "I'm mad about a cremora
tart." — *sjokolade-cremora-marshmellow-tert-vir-joanita*; "I almost ate myself sick on
this treat." — *grondboontjiebotterblokkies*.

## Translation conventions

How the corpus renders her Afrikaans quirks in English. Each point contrasts both sides
(af → en); both quotes are verbatim and grep-verifiable in their respective files.

**Afrikaans dish names stay untranslated inside the prose.** Culturally loaded dish nouns
that anchor the personal writing are kept in Afrikaans, not Englished:

- af "Koolbredie is so n soort kos." → en "Koolbredie is that kind of food." — *koolbredie-o-koolbredie*
- af "Daar is iets aan frikkadelle wat troos." → en "There is something about frikkadelle that comforts." — *frikkadelle-met-kool-sielskos*
- Also kept: "There's something comforting about tamatiekos." — *tamatiekos*; "the bredie
  actually tastes even nicer!" — *guinness-wortelbredie-nou-wat-kan-lekkerder*. Plainly
  descriptive recipe *titles*, by contrast, may be Englished (Coconut Rice, Chocolate Cake).

**"braai" stays "braai"** — never "barbecue" — as verb and noun; "biltong" stays "biltong":

- af "op die vuur ook braai" → en "you can also braai it over the fire" — *varkrib-wat-kleef-en-blink*
- af "Braaibroodjies is die koning van enige braai." → en "Braaibroodjies are the king of any braai." — *braaibroodjies-tradisioneel-en-n-wenner*
- af "rugby en biltong" → en "as much a part of our lives as rugby and biltong" — *aartappelslaai-o-aartappelslaai*

**"Kyk" becomes "Look"** as a direct opening address, keeping the buttonholing tone:

- af "Kyk , aartappelsaai is so deel van ons lewe soos rugby en biltong." → en "Look, potato salad is as much a part of our lives as rugby and biltong." — *aartappelslaai-o-aartappelslaai*

**The stretched-vowel exclamation is preserved phonetically**, by drawing out an English
word rather than normalising it:

- af "maklik en djeeeeeeeeerlik." → en "It's cheap, easy and deeeeeeeelicious." — *ryspoeding-sommer-net-vir-lekker*

**"Maklik!" becomes "Easy!"** — the exclamation of delight, exclamation mark and all:

- af "Bietjie werk, maar maklik!" → en "A bit of work, but easy!" — *botterhoender*

**Idioms are localised, not calqued** — natural English idiom that keeps the warmth:

- af "Ek kan my gesin in n wip vang met die hoender." → en "I can win my family over in a flash with this chicken." — *broccolihoender-my-kinders-se-gunsteling*
- af "smaak vorentoe." → en "taste amazing." — *uie-marmelade-lekker-saam-met-alles*
- af "Ek het amper myself disnis geëet aan dié lekkerte." → en "I almost ate myself sick on this treat." — *grondboontjiebotterblokkies*

**Code-switching: English already embedded in the Afrikaans simply stays English.** Brand
and product names are never "corrected":

- af "gespuit is met Spray and Cook" → en "sprayed with Spray and Cook" — *kondensmelk-melktert-geen-bak-nodig*
- af "Mexican rub gebruik" → en "I used a Mexican rub" — *varkrib-wat-kleef-en-blink*
- "avo" stays "avo" ("Made with avo!!!!!!!!" — *banting-sjokolade-mousse-gemaak-met-avo*), as
  do shop and brand names ("Spekko's Jasmine rice" — *ryspoeding-sommer-net-vir-lekker*; "at Fruit and Veg" — *guinness-wortelbredie-nou-wat-kan-lekkerder*).

**Units and temperatures stay metric and written out**, unchanged from the Afrikaans:

- af "Bak dit vir 40 minute by 180 grade Celsius." → en "Bake it for 40 minutes at 180 degrees Celsius." — *nutellabroodpoeding*

## Don'ts

Things that would ring false — a model that does these does **not** sound like Marinda:

- **Don't over-formalise or stiffen.** She says it herself: "This is no fancy cooking
  blog." — *oor-my*. Avoid a corporate or magazine announcement tone; write like a person
  talking.
- **Don't use generic, boilerplate "AI warmth"** ("Enjoy this delicious recipe with your
  loved ones!"). Her warmth is always **specific and personal** — a cold night in
  Hopefield: "Tonight it's bitterly cold in Hopefield." — *nutellabroodpoeding*. Choose the
  concrete detail over the general feeling.
- **Don't invent family, names or memories.** She dedicates dishes to real people
  ("Sarietha, this one is for you and Josh." — *kool-lensiefrikkadelle*). Never make one up
  — leave those blank if the fact is not in the source.
- **Don't translate away her embedded English or SA vocabulary.** "braai" is not
  "barbecue", and "I used a Mexican rub" — *varkrib-wat-kleef-en-blink* stays as she says
  it; keep the Afrikaans dish names that anchor the prose.
- **Don't tidy her clusters of exclamation marks into one neat full stop.** "So
  delicious!!!!!!!" — *kondensmelk-melktert-geen-bak-nodig* — the excess *is* the voice.
- **Don't end the recipe coldly.** Avoid a dry "Serve warm." Her close binds back to
  feeling: "That's how I make my kids' hearts happy." — *broccolihoender-my-kinders-se-gunsteling*.
